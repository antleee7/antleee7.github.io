Undergraduate Algebra & Geometry Seminar

Organized by Seungjae Yun. This seminar was funded 500,000 KRW through Professor Otto van Koert.

March 4th, 2025

Title: Playing with surfaces

Speaker: Anthony Hojin Lee homepage

Abstract: The talk will revolve around classical theorems in algebraic geometry. I will first introduce Mumford's proof of the famous fact that every smooth cubic surface contains exactly 27 lines. If time permits, I will also prove that there are 12 rational cubic curves that pass through 8 general points in the plane. I will assume very basic familiarity with projective spaces.

March 11th, 2025

Title: Rectifiable Currents and the Plateau Problem

Speaker: Hyunjun Lee

Abstract: The purpose of the talk is to provide an intuitive understanding of how currents can be used to represent rectifiable sets and endow them with a structure of convergence. I will go through the concepts and briefly present how currents were used in solving the Plateau existence problem. Prerequisites will be kept to a minimum.

March 18th, 2025

Title: Intoduction to local fields and applications of Frobenius element

Speaker: Hyomin Park

Abstract: The seminar has two main purposes. First one is to introduce local fields and show two basic (also powerful) properties of extensions of local fields. After presenting a general diagram for galois extension of number fields (involving local fields), I will introduce the Frobenius element with some friendly examples.

April 1st, 2025

Title: An Invitation to $\infty$-categories

Speaker: Seungjae Yun

Abstract: $\infty$-categories arose from the study of model categories and has now become a widely used setup in modern mathematics. The virtue is that they keep track of all the (homotopy) structure on the space of morphisms, so that one may view it as a refinement of the classical setup. This will be the first in a series of talks going through the basic constructions in derived algebraic geometry. Prerequisites for this talk will be basic category theory.

April 10th, 2025

Title: An Introduction to Toric Varieties

Speaker: Donghae Lee

Abstract: Toric varieties and symplectic toric manifolds are introduced as geometric objects equipped with torus actions in the algebraic and symplectic settings, respectively. Their correspondence is described via Delzant’s theorem. No background in algebraic or symplectic geometry is assumed.

April 23rd, 2025

Title: The ADE Classification

Speaker: Anthony Hojin Lee

Abstract: In this talk, I will introduce the simply laced Dynkin diagrams and the so-called ADE classification of various mathematical objects via such diagrams. Some instances that fall under this classification include simply laced Lie algebras, finite subgroups of the spatial rotation group and $\mathrm{SU}(2)$, and consequently the du Val singularities in algebraic geometry.

May 7th, 2025

Title: Introduction to adeles and ideles: relation to classical theorems

Speaker: Hyomin Park

Abstract: In this talk, first I will briefly summarize 4 classical theorems of algebraic number theory(strong approximation of number field, finiteness of class number, Dirichlet's unit theorem, Artin's product formula). Next I will introduce the definition of adeles and ideles, observing how their structural theorems include those 4 classical theorems.

May 13th, 2025

Title: Spin Structures and Orientations in Floer Cohomology

Speaker: Donghae Lee

Abstract: In this talk, I will introduce various definitions of Stiefel–Whitney classes and spin structures. Then I will explain how to assign orientations to moduli spaces used in defining Floer cohomology for spin Lagrangian submanifolds.

May 27th, 2025

Title: An Invitation to Derived Algebraic Geometry

Speaker: Seungjae Yun

Abstract: As a sequel of the previous talk on infinite categories, we will discover how they are applied to derived algebraic geometry. We will aim to understand how derived algebraic geometry overcomes some of the main difficulties in classical algebraic geometry, especially for moduli spaces. This talk will require background on classical/higher stack theory. However for those new to this subject, I will try to explain how to imagine them in a heuristic way.
